The Rise of Quantum Internet Is Closer Than You Think

Scientists achieve breakthrough in quantum entanglement networks

By Dr. Maya Chen
SEPTEMBER 22, 2025 5:09 PM ET

In a breakthrough that brings us one step closer to a quantum internet, researchers at MIT have successfully demonstrated the first stable quantum entanglement network spanning over 100 kilometers. The achievement, published in Nature Physics this week, represents a critical milestone in the development of ultra-secure quantum communication systems that could revolutionize how we transmit sensitive information.

The quantum internet promises capabilities that seem almost magical compared to today's classical networks. Instead of transmitting bits of information as 1s and 0s, quantum networks use quantum bits (qubits) that can exist in multiple states simultaneously. This property, called superposition, combined with quantum entanglement—what Einstein famously called "spooky action at a distance"—enables theoretically unbreakable encryption and instantaneous communication across vast distances.

"What we've achieved is essentially the quantum equivalent of the first internet connection between two computers in 1969," explains Dr. Sarah Kim, lead researcher at MIT's Quantum Information Processing Laboratory. "We're not just sending classical information through quantum channels—we're creating and maintaining entangled states between distant quantum nodes, which is the fundamental building block of quantum networking."

The technical challenges overcome by the team are staggering. Quantum states are notoriously fragile, easily destroyed by environmental interference such as temperature fluctuations, electromagnetic fields, or even cosmic radiation. The researchers developed a novel error correction system that uses diamond-based quantum memory units to store and protect quantum information for extended periods—up to several minutes, an eternity in quantum terms.

The implications for cybersecurity are profound. Current encryption methods rely on mathematical problems that are difficult for classical computers to solve, but quantum computers could potentially crack these codes in hours rather than millennia. A quantum internet, however, would provide quantum key distribution (QKD) that is secured by the laws of physics themselves—any attempt to intercept quantum-encrypted messages would immediately destroy the quantum states, alerting both sender and receiver to the security breach.

Major tech companies and governments worldwide are racing to develop quantum networks. China has already launched a quantum communication satellite and built a 2,000-kilometer quantum network between Beijing and Shanghai. Google, IBM, and startups like IonQ are investing billions in quantum hardware and software development. The European Union has committed €1 billion to quantum technology research through its Quantum Flagship program.

But the quantum internet won't replace the classical internet overnight. Initial applications will likely focus on highly specialized use cases: secure government communications, financial transactions, and scientific research requiring absolute data integrity. "Think of it as a parallel network for our most sensitive information," says Dr. Kim. "Classical internet for cat videos, quantum internet for state secrets."

The timeline for widespread quantum networking remains uncertain, with experts predicting functional metropolitan-area quantum networks within the next decade, and continental or global networks by 2040. However, the recent breakthrough suggests this timeline might be accelerating. As quantum hardware becomes more stable and error rates decrease, the quantum internet is transitioning from science fiction to engineering challenge.

For the average internet user, the quantum revolution will likely be invisible—just as most people don't think about the underlying fiber optic cables and routing protocols that power today's web browsing. But beneath the surface, quantum networking promises to solve some of our most pressing digital security challenges while enabling entirely new forms of distributed quantum computing and sensing applications that we're only beginning to imagine.
